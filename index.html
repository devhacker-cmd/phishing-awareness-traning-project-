Definition
What exactly is phishing?

Phishing is a type of cyberattack where criminals disguise themselves as trusted people or organisations to trick you into revealing sensitive information or taking a harmful action.

🎣

The "fishing" analogy

Just like a fisherman uses bait to lure fish, attackers use fake emails, messages, or websites as "bait" to lure victims. The bait looks real — a bank email, a delivery notification, a colleague's message — but it's a trap designed to steal your credentials, money, or data.

🧠

It's about hacking people, not computers

Phishing doesn't need advanced hacking skills. It exploits human psychology — trust, fear, urgency, and curiosity. Even well-secured systems can be breached if a person inside clicks the wrong link. This is why awareness training is the most effective defence.

Types
6 types of phishing attacks

Phishing isn't just email anymore. Attackers have evolved to use every communication channel available.

Email

Email phishing

Mass emails impersonating banks, delivery services, or government bodies. Sent to millions hoping a percentage clicks.

Targeted

Spear phishing

Personalised attack using your name, employer, or role. Much more convincing than generic emails.

SMS

Smishing

Phishing via text messages — fake delivery alerts, bank OTP requests, or prize notifications with malicious links.

Voice

Vishing

Phone calls from fake "tech support", "banks", or "tax authorities" pressuring you to act immediately.

Executive

Whaling

Spear phishing aimed specifically at senior executives or high-value targets within organisations.

QR Code

Quishing

Fake QR codes in emails, posters, or menus that redirect to phishing sites designed to steal your data.

Real-world examples
What phishing actually looks like

Every example below is annotated so you can see exactly how attackers deceive victims.

Example 1 — Email phishing
⚠ Phishing email
Read the annotations — every red flag is labelled
From:security@paypa1-accounts.net
▶Domain is "paypa1-accounts.net" — the letter "l" replaced with "1". Not PayPal's real domain (paypal.com).
Subject:⚠ URGENT: Your PayPal account has been LIMITED
▶All-caps "URGENT" and "LIMITED" create panic. Legitimate companies don't do this.

Dear Valued Customer,

We have detected suspicious activity on your account. To prevent permanent suspension you must verify your identity within 24 hours.

▶"24 hours" is a pressure tactic to stop you thinking clearly. Real banks always give you time.

Click below to restore access immediately:

Verify My Account Now
▶This button would link to a fake website that steals your username and password.

© PayPal Inc. · 2211 North First Street, San Jose, CA 95131

▶Copying real addresses is trivial. It proves nothing about the sender's legitimacy.
Example 2 — Smishing (SMS)
⚠ Phishing SMS
HDFC-Bank
Your HDFC account has been temporarily blocked due to suspicious activity. Verify now to restore access: http://bit.ly/hdfc-verify9 — Ignore and account closes in 2hrs.
▶Shortened URL hides the real destination. "2hrs" creates urgency. Real HDFC never uses bit.ly links.
HDFCBK (legitimate)
Dear Customer, your a/c XX1234 was debited ₹500 on 23-Jun. If not done by you, call 1800-202-6161. —HDFC Bank
▶Legitimate bank SMS: shows partial account number, gives a real phone number, no links, no urgency.
Example 3 — Vishing (phone call)
⚠ Phishing call script
Caller: "Hello, this is Rahul from SBI fraud department. We've detected a suspicious transaction of ₹45,000 on your account ending 7892. To block this immediately, I need to verify your identity. Can you please share your OTP?"

Red flags:
▶Banks never ask for OTPs over the phone. An OTP is your private code — not even bank employees need it.
▶The urgency ("block this immediately") is designed to panic you into acting without verifying.
▶Always hang up and call your bank directly using the number on the back of your card.
Prevention
How to protect yourself

These habits will dramatically reduce your risk of falling victim to a phishing attack.

1

Always verify the sender's email address

Display names can be faked. Click on the sender name to reveal the real address. Check for typos, extra words, or wrong domains (paypa1.com vs paypal.com).

✓ Quick check: hover over sender name → reveal full address
2

Never click links in unexpected emails

Type the website address directly in your browser instead. Hover over links first — the real destination shows in your browser's status bar.

✓ Rule: when in doubt, go directly to the official website
3

Enable multi-factor authentication (MFA)

Even if attackers steal your password, MFA blocks them from logging in without your second factor (phone OTP, authenticator app). Enable it everywhere.

✓ Priority: email, banking, social media, work accounts
4

Pause before acting on urgency

Urgency is the attacker's most powerful tool. If an email says "act now", take a breath. Legitimate organisations give you time. Call them using a number you find independently.

✓ Mantra: the more urgent it feels, the more suspicious you should be
5

Never share OTPs or passwords with anyone

No bank, company, or government agency will ever ask for your OTP, password, or PIN over phone, email, or SMS. Ever. If someone asks, it's a scam.

✓ India helpline: cybercrime.gov.in · 1930
6

Report suspicious messages

Don't just delete — report. Use your email client's "Report phishing" button. In India, report to cybercrime.gov.in or call 1930. Your report protects others.

✓ Reporting takes 10 seconds and can save someone else
Quick reference
Before you click — checklist

Run through this checklist whenever you receive an unexpected email or message.

✓Is the sender's email address correct?
✓Were you expecting this email?
✓Does the link match where it claims to go?
✓Is there unusual urgency or pressure?
✓Does it ask for personal info or OTPs?
✓Does the greeting use your real name?
✓Are there spelling mistakes or odd phrasing?
✓Is the attachment expected and from a known sender?
